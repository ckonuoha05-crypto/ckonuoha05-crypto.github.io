// Undergraduate Research · Jan 2026 – May 2026 · Saint Louis University

Adaptive Audio-Haptic Feedback System

Research Question

How can vibration-based haptic feedback improve touchscreen navigation, spatial understanding, and target localization for visually impaired users?

Overview

Developed an Android application to evaluate adaptive audio-haptic feedback for visually impaired touchscreen navigation. The system translates spatial information — distance, angle, and positional displacement — into vibration-based feedback guiding users toward a target. The project explored intensity gradients, pattern-based vibration, and directional signaling, grounded in information foraging theory and cognitive map formation research.

Key Technical Work
  • Built Android app in Kotlin generating random target positions and computing real-time spatial relationships
  • Implemented vibration modulation via Android haptic APIs to evaluate perceptual thresholds
  • Designed and compared intensity-based vs. pattern-based feedback approaches
  • Designed calibration protocols for detectable and comfortable vibration limits
  • Integrated text-to-speech (TTS) with multimodal haptic cues
  • Applied spatial cognition and information foraging theory to system architecture
  • Conducted pilot user testing, analyzing navigation behavior and target acquisition strategies
Key Findings
  • Gradient-based (proximity) feedback was more intuitive and reliable than purely directional approaches
  • Pattern-based vibration rhythm outperformed pure intensity modulation for spatial zone distinction
  • Users developed natural navigation strategies — axis-based scanning, screen-edge referencing — without instruction
  • Removing auditory vibration cues (noise-canceling headphones trial) significantly reduced accuracy, revealing an unintended information channel
  • Faster exploratory movement improved target acquisition over slow, over-analyzed navigation
